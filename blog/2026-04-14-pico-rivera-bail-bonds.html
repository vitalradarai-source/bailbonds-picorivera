The Booking Process at LASD Pico Rivera Station — Step by Step

Every LASD booking follows a standardized process administered by custody staff. Here is what happens from the moment of arrest to the point where bail can be posted in Pico Rivera:

Arrival
Transport and Intake
Deputies transport the defendant to the Pico Rivera Station at 6631 Passons Blvd. The arrest report is logged, charges are recorded, and a booking number is assigned. This is the moment the 48-hour arraignment clock starts under California Penal Code § 825.
Property
Personal Property Inventory
All belongings — phone, wallet, keys, jewelry — are catalogued, bagged, and secured. The defendant receives a written receipt. Communication with the outside becomes limited to the station's internal phone system from this point.
Identity
ID Verification and Warrant Search
Deputies confirm identity and run the defendant's name through state and federal databases. Any outstanding warrants from other jurisdictions are flagged here — a warrant from Orange County, San Bernardino, or another jurisdiction can place a hold that prevents immediate release even after Pico Rivera bail is posted.
Biometrics
Live Scan Fingerprinting and Booking Photo
Electronic fingerprints are submitted to the California Department of Justice and the FBI. A booking photo is taken. Criminal history confirmed through Live Scan can influence the bail amount or whether a judge allows bail at arraignment.
Medical
Health Screening
California law requires a medical and mental health screening for every booked individual. Defendants with immediate health needs may be transported to a hospital before booking is fully completed — this can extend the timeline.
Bail Set
Bail Amount Assigned
Bail is set according to the Los Angeles County bail schedule for the specific charges filed. California law imposes no mandatory waiting period after booking — a bail bond can be posted the moment this step is complete.
Housing
Cell Assignment or Transfer
The defendant is assigned to a holding cell at Pico Rivera Station or flagged for transfer to Men's Central Jail or CRDF. Felony defendants, those with medical housing needs, and those with active holds are the most likely to be transferred.

Misdemeanor bookings at LASD Pico Rivera Station typically take 2 to 8 hours from arrival. Felony bookings can run 6 to 16 hours, particularly if a transfer to a county facility is needed. The moment booking is complete and bail is assigned — a bail bond can be posted.

Domestic Violence Bail in Pico Rivera — What the Law Actually Says

Domestic violence arrests are among the most emotionally charged situations families face, and they come with some of the most misunderstood bail rules in California. As a licensed bondsman who has handled hundreds of DV cases throughout Los Angeles County, here is what actually happens under the law — and what does not.

The two most common domestic violence charges in Pico Rivera are:

  • PC 273.5 — Corporal Injury on a Spouse or Cohabitant: This felony charge covers any willful infliction of physical injury on a current or former spouse, cohabitant, or co-parent. Under the 2026 LA County bail schedule, bail is set at $50,000. The 10% bond fee is $5,000.
  • PC 243(e)(1) — Battery on a Cohabitant: This misdemeanor charge covers any unlawful touching of a spouse, cohabitant, or dating partner. Bail is set at $10,000 on the LA County schedule. The 10% bond fee is $1,000.

Additional charges that frequently accompany domestic violence arrests in Pico Rivera include PC 422 (criminal threats), with bail set at $50,000, and PC 646.9 (stalking), with bail set at $150,000.

The 72-Hour Hold Myth — Corrected

One of the most damaging pieces of misinformation circulating about California domestic violence arrests is the claim that there is a mandatory 72-hour hold after arrest. This is not California law. There is no statute requiring a mandatory holding period after a domestic violence arrest before bail can be posted. Once booking is complete and the bail schedule amount is assigned — which can happen within a few hours of arrest — a licensed bail bondsman can post the bond immediately.

What does exist is PC § 136.2, which authorizes a criminal protective order (CPO) — a no-contact order between the defendant and the alleged victim. This protective order is typically issued by the judge at arraignment, not at the time of arrest. The CPO is separate from the bail decision entirely. A person can be released on a bail bond and still have a protective order in place — compliance with the order is a condition of their release.

Under the California Constitution, Article I, Section 12, bail can only be denied by a judge in specific narrow circumstances — capital crimes with strong evidence of guilt, or felonies with a showing of likelihood of great bodily harm to others. Standard domestic violence misdemeanor and felony charges do not automatically fall into these categories. Most defendants are entitled to have bail set and posted.

Key facts about the Norwalk Courthouse for bail bond purposes:

  • Arraignment timing: Under PC § 825, arraignment must occur within 48 hours of arrest, excluding Sundays and court holidays. If your loved one is arrested on a Thursday night and the 48-hour window falls on a Saturday, arraignment is pushed to the next court day — Monday.   • Court date compliance: Failure to appear at any scheduled Norwalk Courthouse hearing can result in the court issuing a bench warrant and the bail bond being forfeited.   • Bond exoneration: When the case is resolved — through dismissal, plea, or verdict — the bond is exonerated and no further obligations remain under the bail contract.
